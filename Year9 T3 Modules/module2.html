Fluids

Learning Focus

By the end of this toolkit, you will be able to:

  • Differentiate fluid power systems: Understand the difference between hydraulics and pneumatics using Pascal's principle and pressure calculations.
  • Calculate hydraulic forces: Calculate output forces in hydraulic systems using piston areas and real syringe data.
  • Build and test: Plan, build and test a hydraulic digger while managing leaks, friction and safety controls.

Key Notes

Why this matters: Understanding fluid power systems is essential for many engineering applications, from construction equipment to manufacturing automation.

1. Introduction to Fluid Power

Hydraulics use liquids and pneumatics use gases, but both rely on Pascal's principle: pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions. Pressure is defined as P = F/A, meaning a force on a small piston can generate a larger force on a bigger piston.

2. Calculating Force in Hydraulic Systems

Different piston areas create mechanical advantage following F2 = F1 × (A2/A1). Syringes with larger diameters on the load side multiply the input force, while travel distances change according to the area ratio.

3. Building and Analysing the Hydraulic Digger

Design the excavator arm by selecting lever lengths and cylinder placements that create the desired reach and bucket motion. Measure input force and displacement at the syringes, record output force and movement at the bucket, and evaluate efficiency.

4. Fluid Behaviour, Friction and Leakage

Liquids are nearly incompressible, but air bubbles or flexible tubing absorb movement and reduce responsiveness. Friction between plungers and barrels plus leakage at joints lowers efficiency; compare hydraulics to pneumatics for response and control.

5. Risk Management in Fluid-Power Projects

Hazards include: popping syringes, water spills and sharp edges during frame construction. Use water instead of oil, limit plunger force, keep electrical equipment away from spills, and wear safety glasses.

6. Communication and Evaluation

Document the hydraulic digger with a design brief, sketches, hydraulic calculations, photos, test data tables/graphs, and reflective evaluation. Summarise how design changes affect force, reach and control.

Activities & Evidence

Complete these activities to demonstrate your understanding:

  • Design phase: Sketch cylinder placement options and calculate expected output forces using syringe diameters.
  • Construction phase: Assemble the digger frame, bleed air from the system and record travel distances for each joint.
  • Testing phase: Test load lifting capacity, compare theoretical vs measured forces and calculate efficiency.
  • Documentation: Create a mini-report with risk controls, photos, data tables and a reflection on improvements.

Q&A

How does Pascal's principle apply to hydraulics?
Any pressure applied at one point in the enclosed fluid is transmitted equally throughout, so a small force on a small piston can generate a larger force on a larger piston.
Why use different syringe diameters?
Changing piston area changes the force and distance trade-off. A larger output syringe multiplies force but moves a shorter distance compared with the input syringe.
What reduces efficiency in a hydraulic digger?
Air bubbles, flexible tubing, plunger friction and leaks reduce force transfer and responsiveness, lowering actual output compared with theory.
How should hazards be managed during testing?
Use clamps, keep electrics away from water, avoid over-pressurising syringes, wear safety glasses and clean spills immediately to maintain WHS compliance.
