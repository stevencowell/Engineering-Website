Hydraulic Digger Project Instructions

Project brief

Design, build and test a tabletop hydraulic digger using syringes/tubing (fluid power) and lever linkages (mechanisms), then evaluate how your design choices affected performance.

  • Functions: Build a digger that controls boom, dipper and bucket (three separate circuits).
  • Fluid power: Use Pascal’s principle to calculate expected force multiplication (P = F/A).
  • Mechanisms: Use lever ratios + pivot placement to create the reach and lift you want.
  • Build quality: Accurate pivot holes, smooth movement, strong joints, no binding.
  • Hydraulic setup: Fill with water and remove air (air = spongy system and poor results).
  • Testing: Test systematically and record input force + movement vs load lifted + movement.
  • Evidence: Design drawings + build log photos + test tables/graphs + evaluation & improvements.

What “good” looks like: clear drawings (with dimensions), neat build, tight connections, repeatable testing, and a folio that links results back to theory.

Materials & evidence

  • Timber/ply for base and arm sections; fasteners; pivots (bolts/dowels) and washers/spacers.
  • Syringes (e.g., 5–10 mL input; 20–50 mL output) + clear tubing; cable ties/clamps for leak-free joins.
  • Water + towels/drip tray (keep benches safe and dry).
  • Test gear: spring balance/force meter, weights, ruler/tape, protractor (if measuring angles).
  • Safety: eye protection, safe drilling/cutting setup, clear test zone.

Hand in: a folio/report with your brief & criteria, drawings (hand or CAD), calculations, build log photos, test data (tables/graphs), analysis (predicted vs actual), evaluation and improvements.

Build steps

1
Interpret the brief & plan evidence

Identify requirements (functions, safety, testing) and what evidence you must capture for your folio.

More tips
  • Highlight constraints: number of circuits (boom/dipper/bucket), size limits (if given), and safety rules.
  • Start a one-page checklist: drawings done, calculations done, build photos, test tables/graphs, evaluation.
  • Decide your test method early (what will you measure, and how will you keep it consistent?).

Quick check: Can you point to the exact evidence you need for each stage (design, build, test)?

2
Design the digger & hydraulics

Sketch the arm geometry, pivots, lever lengths and cylinder attachment points. Select syringe sizes and predict forces.

More tips
  • Use F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁) to predict force multiplication. Always calculate using areas (πd²/4).
  • Levers change force and speed: longer output arm increases movement but reduces force.
  • Plan three circuits: boom lift (highest load), dipper (reach), bucket (control).
  • Annotate your sketch: dimensions, pivot locations, syringe diameters, lever lengths.

Quick check: Does your design clearly show pivots, lever lengths, and where each cylinder attaches?

3
Cut parts & drill pivots accurately

Use your cutting plan, label parts, and drill pivot holes square and aligned.

More tips
  • Mark from a datum edge (reduces cumulative error).
  • Misaligned pivot holes cause binding and low efficiency; use a drill press or guide if possible.
  • Dry-fit arm sections before final assembly and fix any interference early.
  • Remove sharp edges (sanding) and check clearances around moving parts.

Quick check: Do all arm sections swing freely by hand before the hydraulics go on?

4
Assemble frame, arms & pivots

Assemble base, boom, dipper and bucket. Install pivots with spacers/washers to prevent rubbing.

More tips
  • Check each joint is square and stiff; loose joints create lost motion.
  • Use washers/spacers to stop timber-on-timber friction at pivots.
  • Record any build changes from your design (and why) with photos for the folio.

Quick check: Can the boom/dipper/bucket move through full range without catching?

5
Plumb circuits & remove air

Connect tubing and syringes, fill with water, and bleed all air so the system feels firm and responsive.

More tips
  • Secure tubing with clamps/cable ties; leaks kill pressure and data reliability.
  • Avoid sharp bends/kinks and keep tubing lengths reasonable (less friction).
  • Cycle each cylinder through full travel multiple times; continue until bubbles stop.
  • Mount syringes with brackets so they can move freely without binding.

Quick check: Does the output move immediately when you push the input (no sponginess)?

6
Test, record & evaluate

Secure the digger, test each function systematically, and compare predicted vs measured MA/VR and efficiency.

More tips
  • Measure input force (spring balance) and output force (load lifted). Record displacement for input and output.
  • Create a table: Test #, input force, output force, theoretical MA, actual MA, efficiency (%).
  • Use the same setup each trial: same load point (bucket centre), same measurement points, same increments.
  • Explain differences: air, friction, leakage, pivot alignment, lever geometry.
  • Propose at least two specific improvements (e.g., adjust lever ratio, change syringe size, stiffen joints).

Quick check: Do you have photos/video of the test setup and a clear data table + graph?

Quick checks

  • Do your drawings show dimensions, pivots, lever lengths and cylinder attachment points?
  • Do you have three independent hydraulic circuits (boom/dipper/bucket)?
  • Are all pivots aligned (no binding) and all joints firm (no wobble)?
  • Is the hydraulic system filled with water and fully de-aired (no spongy feel)?
  • Is your test method repeatable (same load point and measurement points each trial)?

Task Overview

Design, build and test a hydraulic digger with lever linkages. Document your complete process from design through testing and evaluation in a comprehensive project folio.

Key Requirements:

  • Apply fluid power principles (Pascal’s principle, pressure, force and area relationships)
  • Design digger with lever linkages and hydraulic circuits
  • Build the digger safely and accurately
  • Conduct systematic testing and calculate MA/VR and efficiency
  • Compare predicted vs measured performance and explain differences

Term 3 Focus: Fluid Power & Mechanisms

Term 3 focuses on fluid power and mechanisms through the Hydraulic Digger project. You will learn about Pascal’s principle, lever linkages, mechanical advantage (MA), velocity ratio (VR) and efficiency while building and testing a functional hydraulic control system.

  • Use theory to predict force multiplication and movement trade-offs.
  • Build a reliable hydraulic system (tight connections, no air).
  • Test in a controlled way and justify design decisions with data.
