Design

Learning Focus

Students turn the energy context into a practical design. The aim is not just to make something spin, but to design a compact system that supports the impellor, transfers rotation cleanly and protects the generator and USB output.

Design Constraints

  • Maximum size: 250 x 150 x 100 mm
  • Stable frame and secure rotating parts
  • Teacher-checked low-voltage output
  • Clear access to generator and USB unit

Design Choices

  • Blade number, shape, pitch and material
  • Direct drive or gear/cog transfer
  • Shaft support, bearings and alignment
  • Frame material and fixing method

Design Problem: Catch Wind Without Wasting It

A strong design converts moving air into smooth shaft rotation. The impellor needs enough surface area and blade angle to start rotating, but it also needs to stay balanced and light. A large, heavy blade set may look powerful but fail because it needs too much wind to start.

High-Drag Wind Catcher

Uses cups, scoops or broad surfaces to catch air. It can start easily at low speed, but it may rotate slowly and create more vibration.

Lift-Style Blade

Uses angled or aerofoil-like blades. It can spin faster once moving, but it is harder to make accurately and may need better alignment.

Design judgement: The best classroom impellor is not always the most realistic wind turbine. It is the one that works safely with the available generator, wind source, materials, build time and size limit.

Direct Drive, Gears And Cogs

The assessment specifically mentions gears and cogs. Students should understand that gearing is not magic. A gear train can increase generator speed, change direction or make layout easier, but it also adds friction and alignment problems.

Drive optionAdvantagesDisadvantagesWhen to choose it
Direct driveSimple, fewer parts, less friction, easier to align.Generator speed depends directly on impellor speed.When the impellor can spin the generator fast enough without gearing.
Gear-up driveCan make the generator spin faster than the impellor.Reduces torque, adds friction, may stop the impellor starting.When the impellor has enough torque but generator speed is too low.
Gear-down driveIncreases torque and may help under load.Generator spins slower, which may reduce voltage.Rare for this charger, but useful to discuss as a trade-off.
Belt or pulley driveCan tolerate slight misalignment and isolate vibration.Can slip and may be harder to fit in the size limit.As an extension if gears are binding or too noisy.
Simple gear language: If a large impellor gear drives a small generator gear, the generator spins faster but with less torque. If a small gear drives a large gear, speed reduces but torque increases.

Worked Design Example

Problem: A student has a 90 mm four-blade impellor that spins steadily, but the generator output is weak. The impellor has enough torque to keep spinning under light load.

  1. Hypothesis: The generator may need higher rotational speed.
  2. Design change: Try a gear-up arrangement, such as a larger gear on the impellor shaft driving a smaller gear on the generator shaft.
  3. Risk: Added friction may stop the impellor starting, or poor gear mesh may create vibration.
  4. Fair test: Keep the same wind source and blade design, then compare direct drive and geared drive output.
  5. Evidence: Record voltage/output, starting behaviour, noise, vibration and whether parts stayed aligned.

Materials And Construction Choices

The assignment template asks students to discuss materials used in wind turbine blades and solar panels, including strength, weight, efficiency, recyclability and disposal. For this prototype, material choice should be scaled down to classroom reality.

Blade Materials

  • Card: quick to test, light, easy to modify, poor durability.
  • Plastic sheet: light and water resistant, can crack or flex too much.
  • Thin ply: stiffer and stronger, heavier and harder to shape.
  • 3D printed: accurate and repeatable, but can be slow and may need support material.
  • Laser cut: accurate flat parts, but blade pitch must still be formed or built into the design.

Frame Materials

  • Plywood/MDF: easy to cut and assemble; must be kept square.
  • Acrylic: neat presentation; can crack if drilled poorly.
  • Cardboard prototypes: useful for concept testing but weak for final testing.
  • 3D printed brackets: accurate mounting but can fail along layer lines.
  • Fasteners/adhesives: should resist vibration and allow safe inspection.

WHS Design Controls

Safety starts in the design, not after the model is built. Rotating parts, cutting/drilling, small shafts, sharp edges, unstable frames and low-voltage testing all need controls.

HazardRiskDesign control
Fast spinning impellorContact with fingers, hair, clothing or wires.Place the impellor away from hands, keep a clear test zone, and avoid loose leads near blades.
Unbalanced shaftVibration, loose parts, generator damage.Use accurate hubs, balanced blades and supported shafts.
Gear/cog pinch pointsFingers or wires caught between teeth.Keep gears inside the frame, mark no-touch zones and stop testing before adjustments.
Low-voltage electrical outputDamaged components or device if connected incorrectly.Teacher check, test meter first, confirm polarity and avoid personal phone testing until approved.

  • Record the generator body size, shaft size, available bearings and overall size limit.
  • Draw a labelled box showing the maximum 250 x 150 x 100 mm volume.
  • List what must fit inside the volume.

2. Impellor Investigation

  • Compare at least three blade or wind catcher ideas.
  • Predict which will start easiest, spin fastest and stay most stable.
  • Explain one sustainability or material-choice issue for each option.

3. Gear/Cog Decision

  • Sketch direct drive and one geared option.
  • Explain the speed/torque trade-off in plain language.
  • Choose which option you will build first and why.

4. Concept Evaluation Table

  • Insert sketches or photos of at least five concepts where possible.
  • Write strengths and weaknesses for each concept.
  • Choose the final design using evidence from function, safety, size, materials and build quality.

Common Mistakes To Avoid

  • Oversized blades: if the model does not fit the size limit, it fails the brief even if it spins.
  • Ignoring the 2 mm shaft: a loose hub or heavy impellor can bend or slip on the shaft.
  • Adding gears without testing: gears can reduce output if they add more friction than speed benefit.
  • No real design choice: students must justify why one concept is better than another.
  • Weak frame: a flexible frame ruins alignment and makes test data unreliable.

Toolkit Self-Check

Why is alignment important between the impellor, shaft and generator?

Poor alignment increases friction, vibration and wear. It can reduce electrical output, loosen fixings and make the impellor wobble during testing. Good alignment helps the system spin smoothly and makes test results more reliable.

What design features help reduce friction in the charger prototype?

Useful features include straight shaft supports, accurate holes, smooth bearing surfaces, light but stiff blades, careful spacing, secure generator mounting and avoiding parts rubbing on the frame. Less friction means more wind energy reaches the generator.

What could go wrong if the frame flexes during testing?

A flexible frame can move the shaft out of alignment, increase vibration, let blades hit the frame and make electrical output inconsistent. It can also make the model unstable on the bench, which increases the risk during rotating tests.

Why might a gear or cog system improve performance, and why might it make performance worse?

Gears can change speed or torque to suit the generator, which may improve output. They can also add friction, backlash, noise, alignment problems and extra failure points, so students need to test whether the gear train actually helps.

How will you prove that your final design is better than your first idea?

Use evidence, not vibes. Compare sketches, explain why the final option meets the size, stability, safety and output requirements better, then support the decision with test data, photos, teacher feedback or a design decision table.

Why should rotating parts be guarded, spaced or positioned carefully during design?

Rotating blades and shafts can catch fingers, hair, loose clothing or wires. Careful spacing, clear test zones and secure mounting reduce contact risk and stop leads or frame parts from being dragged into the spinning impellor.

What should be included in the folio to justify the selected design?

The folio should include concept sketches, labelled design options, constraints, materials, a final selected design and a short justification explaining why the chosen solution is safer, more stable and more likely to generate useful output.

Why is the 250 x 150 x 100 mm size limit an engineering constraint rather than just a classroom instruction?

A constraint forces design decisions. The charger must fit the available space while still holding the impellor, shaft, generator and USB module safely. Meeting the limit shows the design is controlled, compact and buildable.
