Graphics

Learning Focus

This toolkit turns the selected design into clear technical communication. A strong drawing should let someone understand the size, shape, parts and assembly of the wind-powered USB charger before it is built.

Required Drawing Evidence

  • Overall size and key dimensions
  • Frame, impellor, shaft, generator and USB unit labelled
  • Material choices shown or listed
  • Important fixing points and supports identified

Quality Checks

  • Lines are neat and readable
  • Dimensions use consistent units
  • Parts match the final design, not an early sketch
  • Assembly makes sense from the drawing

Drawing Workflow

  1. Start from your selected design, not your roughest brainstorm.
  2. Draw the front or side view that best shows the impellor, shaft and generator.
  3. Add dimensions for the frame size, blade diameter, shaft position and generator location.
  4. Label each main component and material.
  5. Add a short note explaining how the moving parts connect.
Folio evidence: Add your final drawing or Onshape screenshot, plus a short explanation of how the drawing helped you build accurately.

What A Good Drawing Must Communicate

A good drawing answers build questions before anyone cuts, drills, prints or glues. It should show the final charger as a system: wind input, rotating parts, generator, frame, USB output, supports and safe clearances.

Function

  • Where wind enters the impellor.
  • How rotation reaches the generator.
  • Where output leaves through the USB module.
  • Which parts move and which parts stay fixed.

Construction

  • Overall dimensions and size limit.
  • Frame shape, material and thickness.
  • Shaft centreline and bearing/support positions.
  • Generator mount and fixing method.

Essential Views

Students do not need a professional engineering drawing package, but they do need enough views to build from. One pretty isometric picture is not enough if it hides the shaft, generator or USB module.

ViewWhat it should showCommon mistake
Front viewImpellor diameter, blade position, frame width and shaft height.Drawing blades decoratively without useful dimensions.
Side viewGenerator position, shaft length, frame depth and USB module placement.Hiding the generator behind the impellor.
Top viewOverall footprint, support spacing and wire route.Forgetting the 250 x 150 mm base limit.
Detail viewHub, shaft, bearing or gear/cog connection.Leaving the most difficult connection to guesswork.
Assembly viewHow major parts fit together.Showing parts floating without fixings or supports.

Dimensioning The Charger

Dimensions should be useful. Students should not dimension every tiny line, but they must show the measurements that affect fit, alignment and construction.

DimensionWhy it matters
Overall length, width and heightProves the design fits inside 250 x 150 x 100 mm.
Impellor diameterControls wind capture and clearance inside the frame.
Shaft centre heightControls alignment between impellor and generator.
Generator mount positionControls friction, support and access for wiring.
Hole positionsCritical for laser-cut, drilled or 3D-printed parts.
Material thicknessAffects strength, weight, clearances and assembly fit.
USB module positionPrevents leads from snagging and supports safe testing.
Teacher check: If a student cannot point to the drawing and explain exactly where the generator, shaft, impellor and USB module go, the drawing is not finished.

Onshape / CAD Evidence

The Drive folio explicitly asks for an Onshape design. Onshape evidence can be simple, but it must show design intent. A screenshot of a random shape is not enough.

Minimum CAD Evidence

  • Named file or screenshot showing the final design.
  • Main frame and impellor represented clearly.
  • Generator and USB module shown or labelled.
  • Dimensions visible or included in the drawing export.
  • Short explanation of how CAD helped the build.

Extension CAD Evidence

  • Separate printable or laser-cut parts.
  • Assembly showing how components fit together.
  • Accurate shaft/hub detail.
  • Material thickness and tab/slot details.
  • Revised version after testing feedback.

Drawing For Manufacture

If laser cutting or 3D printing is used, the drawing needs to support manufacture. Digital fabrication can improve marks when it improves accuracy, repeatability, fit or presentation. It should not be used as decorative theatre.

ProcessDrawing/CAD requirementRisk to avoid
Laser cuttingFlat profiles, hole locations, material thickness, tab/slot fit and kerf allowance where needed.Slots too tight, holes missing or parts too weak around corners.
3D printingHub diameter, shaft hole, wall thickness, print orientation and support needs.Weak layer direction, poor hole fit or heavy blade parts.
Hand cutting/drillingClear centre marks, hole sizes, cutting lines and clamping allowance.Inaccurate holes causing friction and misalignment.
AssemblyExploded or labelled view showing order of parts and fixing methods.Gluing parts in a way that prevents testing or repair.

Activities And Folio Evidence

1. Drawing Audit

  • Use the checklist: overall dimensions, impellor diameter, shaft centreline, generator mount, USB position, materials and fixings.
  • Mark any missing details before moving to construction.

2. Final Drawing / Onshape Export

  • Create a final drawing from the selected design, not from an early concept.
  • Include dimensions and labels that would help another student build the same charger.
  • Insert the final drawing or screenshot into the folio.

3. Build-From-Drawing Test

  • Give the drawing to a partner and ask them to identify the build sequence.
  • If they need to guess, add labels, dimensions or a detail view.
  • Record one improvement made to the drawing after peer feedback.

4. Drawing Reflection

  • Explain how the drawing helped prevent a build problem.
  • Use technical language: dimension, clearance, alignment, support, assembly, component and tolerance.

Common Mistakes To Avoid

  • No scale or dimensions: the design may look good but cannot be checked against the size limit.
  • Missing generator detail: the hardest part of the design is often the generator/shaft connection.
  • One view only: one view usually hides depth, wiring route or support details.
  • CAD screenshot without explanation: students must explain why the final design was chosen.
  • Drawing after building: the drawing should guide construction, not pretend to document decisions after the fact.

Toolkit Self-Check

What dimensions are essential for the wind-powered USB charger drawing?

Essential dimensions include the overall frame size, blade or impellor diameter, shaft position, generator location, USB module position and key fixing points. These dimensions show whether the design fits the project limit and can be built accurately.

Why should the generator location be shown clearly on the final drawing?

The generator location controls shaft alignment, wiring route, frame support and access for testing. If it is unclear, the model may be built with extra friction, weak support or unsafe wiring near moving parts.

How can a technical drawing reveal a problem before construction starts?

A drawing can reveal missing space, poor alignment, weak supports, blade clearance problems, awkward fixing points or parts that do not fit inside the size limit. Finding these issues early saves materials and reduces unsafe rework.

What labels would help another student understand your charger design?

Useful labels include impellor, shaft, frame, supports, generator, USB output, wiring route, fixing points, materials and wind direction. Labels should explain both the parts and how energy moves through the system.

Why is it risky to build from a rough brainstorm sketch instead of a final technical drawing?

A brainstorm sketch may not include accurate size, alignment, materials or assembly details. Building from it can lead to wasted material, unsafe changes, poor fit and a prototype that does not match the intended design.

How do drawing standards support WHS during construction?

Clear drawings reduce guesswork. They show where to cut, drill, clamp, support and assemble parts before tools are used. This lowers the chance of rushed decisions, unstable parts and unsafe last-minute changes.

What folio evidence should show that your drawing helped the build?

Include the final drawing or Onshape screenshot, labelled dimensions, a materials or parts list, and a short reflection explaining how the drawing guided accurate construction or helped fix a design issue before building.

Why must the wiring route be considered on the drawing, even though it is not a structural part?

Wiring affects safety and reliability. Leads need to stay clear of rotating parts, sharp edges and strain points. Planning the route on the drawing helps prevent snagging, loose connections and difficult testing access.
