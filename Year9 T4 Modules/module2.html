Core Concepts

Assemblies, Mates & Configurations

This toolkit extends your work from earlier terms by taking the lolly dispenser from a collection of parts to a fully defined, buildable digital product.

Assembly Strategy

  • Sub-assemblies: Group related parts into logical sub-assemblies: e.g. gear train, cam/gate mechanism, frame & base, guard & lid.
  • Mate references: Use simple, robust mates (fasten, revolute, slider) referencing flat faces and axes, not random edges.
  • Motion check: Use limits and interference checking to ensure gears, cams and gates operate without collisions.

Configurations & Variables

  • Variables: Define global variables for thickness, shaft diameters, key clearances (e.g. #shaft_dia, #ply_thickness).
  • Configurations: Use configs for different versions (e.g. small/medium/large, gear ratio options) without cloning entire models.
  • Naming: Name configurations and variables clearly so someone else can read and adjust your model later.

Drawings & Communication

  • Use front/top/side views plus a suitable isometric view for assemblies.
  • Dimension overall size first, then critical fits (shaft centres, gear spacing, guard clearances).
  • Add section views where internal features matter (e.g., shaft through frame, cam profile, overlapping plates).
  • Complete the title block: part/assembly name, scale, units, material, revision, drafter.

Exports & Files

  • Export 2D profiles (DXF/DWG) from sketches for laser/CNC cutting.
  • Check units (mm) and line types, and note any kerf allowance or offset needed.
  • Save exports into clearly named folders (e.g. LaserExports, DrawingsPDF, SourceCAD).
Workflow

Practical Workflow & Checkpoints

Use this sequence as a checklist while you work through the CAD tasks. Capture screenshots and short clips as evidence.

  1. Organise your Onshape document.
    • Rename your document and main tabs (Part Studios, Assemblies, Drawings) using clear, consistent names.
    • Group dispenser-related tabs together; archive tests/old versions into a separate folder or tab group.
  2. Complete key parts and name features.
    • Finish modelling any missing parts (e.g., gate, handle, guard, spacers) using sensible feature trees.
    • Name important features (e.g., gear_boss, shaft_holes, guard_cutout) so you can find them later.
  3. Build sub-assemblies.
    • Create assemblies for the gear train and cam/gate mechanism.
    • Apply mates so the parts move realistically (revolute for shafts, fasten for fixed joints).
    • Use limits if needed to prevent over-travel that would break the mechanism.
  4. Assemble the full dispenser.
    • Insert sub-assemblies into a top-level assembly.
    • Mate to the frame/base and check clearances: nothing should collide or bind through a full cycle.
    • Capture a short motion study or screen recording of the gears/cam operating.
  5. Create build-ready drawings.
    • Create drawings for at least: the main frame, base, gear train sub-assembly, and cam/gate mechanism.
    • Dimension critical features and include at least one section or detail view where needed.
    • Fill in the title block fully, including revision and material details.
  6. Export DXF/DWG and compile your CAD pack.
    • Export DXF/DWG profiles for parts likely to be laser/CNC cut (plates, guards, side panels).
    • Save PDFs of your drawings and link them in a short “Readme” or cover sheet.
    • Check that file names match the part/drawing titles to avoid confusion in the workshop.

Key Notes

Key Notes & Common Mistakes

Assembly & Mates

  • Keep it simple: Use the simplest mate that does the job. Over-complicated mate schemes are harder to debug when something breaks.
  • Reference good geometry: Mate to main faces, axes and planes rather than small edges or corners that may change later.
  • Test motion early: After each mate, drag parts to check for unexpected locking or collisions.

Drawings & Dimensions

  • Avoid “dimension overload” – show enough to build the part accurately, not every single edge.
  • Dimension from consistent datums (e.g., base edge, centreline) to avoid accumulated error.
  • Check that your views are not hiding critical features behind other parts; use section/detail views where needed.

Exports & Version Control

  • Always export after you finish major edits – otherwise the CAD and physical parts will not match.
  • Include the revision or date in file names (e.g., FramePlate_R1.dxf or FramePlate_2025-T4.dxf).
  • Run a test import into the target software (laser/CNC) before committing to a full class set of parts.

Common Mistakes

  • Leaving parts “floating” in the assembly with no fixed reference, leading to random movement.
  • Stacking too many mates on the same pair of parts, causing conflicts or over-constrained assemblies.
  • Dimensioning to hidden or temporary features instead of stable faces or centre marks.
  • Exporting DXF from the wrong view or without checking units, leading to parts at the wrong scale.
Quiz

Check Your Understanding – Onshape & CAD

Use these questions to test your understanding. Click “Show answer” to reveal one possible solution or explanation.

1. Why is it good practice to create sub-assemblies (e.g., gear train, cam/gate) instead of putting every part directly into one giant assembly?
Sub-assemblies make the model easier to manage, understand and troubleshoot. They allow you to test and fix smaller mechanisms (like the gear train) before combining everything. This leads to clearer mates, faster performance and simpler drawings for each mechanism.
2. Give one advantage of using variables and configurations instead of manually editing dimensions every time you want a new size or version.
Variables and configurations keep all versions linked to a single “master” design. Changing the variable updates all related features, reducing the chance of forgetting a dimension and creating inconsistent parts. It saves time and reduces errors when trying alternative sizes or gear ratios.
3. What are two things that must be clear on a build-ready drawing for a frame plate?
Example answers:
  • Overall dimensions (height, width, thickness) of the plate.
  • Exact positions and diameters of shaft holes and fixing holes.
  • Material and thickness noted in the title block or as a note.
  • Any critical tolerances or fits for shafts and bearings.
Any two of these, clearly shown, help the builder avoid guesswork.
4. Why is it risky to export DXF/DWG files early and then keep changing the model without updating the exports?
The exported files will no longer match the latest version of the CAD model. If you cut parts from old DXF files, they may not fit with the updated design, wasting material and time. Always re-export after major changes and clearly label the latest revision.
5. A mate in your assembly is over-constraining a gear and causing it to “lock up”. What are two reasonable troubleshooting steps?
Possible steps include:
  • Suppress or delete mates one at a time until the gear moves freely; then rebuild using fewer, simpler mates.
  • Check that you have not combined multiple mates that control the same degree of freedom (for example, two different mates trying to set the same angle).
  • Ensure the gear is mated to appropriate reference geometry (axes and flat faces) rather than edges that move or change.
6. What is one thing you can do in your CAD pack to make life easier for a technician or teacher who wants to cut your parts on a laser or CNC?
Example answers:
  • Include a short “Readme” or cover sheet explaining which files to cut, what material/thickness to use, and any kerf or offset notes.
  • Use clear, consistent file names that match the drawing titles (e.g., FramePlate.dxf + FramePlate.pdf).
  • Organise exports into labelled folders (e.g., LaserExports, DrawingsPDF) so they do not have to search through unrelated files.
