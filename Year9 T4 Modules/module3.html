Mechanisms

Learning Focus

By the end of this toolkit, you should be able to:

  • Explain how gear ratios, torque and speed affect the operation of your lolly dispenser.
  • Systematically test the mechanism, collect data and identify where jams, misfeeds or failures occur.
  • Adjust clearances, alignments and materials to improve reliability and user experience.
  • Conduct simple user trials and record feedback on ergonomics, effort, speed and consistency.
  • Document changes and justify design decisions using engineering language and evidence.

Core Concepts

Performance, Reliability & User Experience

This toolkit focuses on how well your dispenser actually works in real use, not just whether it matches the drawing.

Gear Ratio, Torque & Speed

  • Gear ratio: Input gear teeth : output gear teeth. A higher ratio can increase torque but reduce output speed.
  • Torque vs effort: More torque makes it easier to turn under load, but the handle may need more turns per dispense.
  • Smooth rotation: Good alignment, low friction and correct clearances are as important as the ratio itself.

Cams, Gates & Feed

  • Cam profile: Controls how quickly and how far the gate moves. Steep profiles can cause sudden forces and jams.
  • Gate clearance: Too tight and it jams; too loose and multiple lollies may escape at once.
  • Gravity & friction: Surface finish, slope angles and material choice affect how reliably lollies feed.

Systematic Testing

  • Test the mechanism in small steps: no load (empty), partial load, full load.
  • Record what happens, not just “works” or “doesn’t work” – note sounds, sticking points, extra effort, misalignment.
  • Change only one variable at a time (e.g. clearance, spring tension, finish) so you know what caused improvement.

User Experience & Safety

  • Consider how it feels to use: handle size, required force, number of turns for a dispense.
  • Check that moving parts are guarded and that fingers are kept clear of pinch points.
  • Observe how users naturally interact with the dispenser – do they instinctively use it correctly?
Testing Workflow

Mechanism Testing & Tuning – Step-by-Step

Use this sequence as a structured process. Record test runs and changes in your folio.

  1. Pre-test inspection.
    • Check all screws, shafts and fixings are tight; no obvious cracks or loose parts.
    • Rotate the mechanism slowly by hand with no lollies to feel for rough spots or binding.
  2. Baseline test – no load.
    • Count how many turns of the handle are required for one full cycle of the cam/gate.
    • Note any positions where resistance increases or the mechanism “clicks” or jumps.
  3. Loaded test – realistic conditions.
    • Load the dispenser with the intended lollies (or an agreed test object) to a normal level.
    • Run at least 10 dispense cycles and record for each: success, partial dispense, jam, double dispense.
    • Take photos or a short video if a particular failure repeats.
  4. Identify patterns & possible causes.
    • Look for patterns (e.g. jamming only when nearly empty, or only when the handle is turned quickly).
    • Link each symptom to a possible cause: clearance, alignment, surface finish, gear ratio, cam profile.
  5. Plan and apply adjustments.
    • Choose one change at a time (e.g. adjust gate clearance, add chamfers, slightly reposition a pivot).
    • Record the change clearly in your folio, including measurements or notes on what was altered.
  6. Re-test and compare results.
    • Repeat the same 10-cycle test and compare success rates to your baseline.
    • Decide whether to keep the change, modify it further, or revert to the previous version.
  7. User trials.
    • Have at least 3 other people try the dispenser (under supervision).
    • Ask them about ease of use, comfort, effort required and how predictable the dispense is.
    • Note any unexpected behaviours (e.g. trying to turn backwards, pulling instead of turning, etc.).
Folio & Evidence

Activities & Evidence

Include the following in your folio or digital submission for Toolkit 3.

  • A simple test table showing at least 10 baseline dispense attempts (success, jam, double dispense, other).
  • Annotated photos or screenshots showing at least two problem areas in the mechanism (e.g. jam points, misalignment, scuffing).
  • A short cause-and-effect table linking each symptom to a likely cause and one planned adjustment.
  • Before-and-after notes or images for at least one successful adjustment (e.g. improved clearance, chamfered edge, guide added).
  • A summary of user trial feedback (3+ users) covering effort, comfort, predictability and overall satisfaction.
  • A short reflection paragraph: What is the main limitation of my current design, and what would be my next improvement if I had more time?
Key Notes

Key Notes & Common Mistakes

Mechanism Behaviour

  • Consistent motion: Mechanisms should feel smooth and predictable; sudden jumps often indicate a geometry or clearance issue.
  • Free vs controlled: Too much friction causes jams; too little control can cause double dispenses or uncontrolled motion.
  • Loads change things: A mechanism that feels fine empty may behave very differently under load.

Testing & Data

  • “It works” is not enough – you need numbers (success rate) and observations to support your design decisions.
  • Always repeat tests using the same procedure so results are comparable.
  • Record adjustments and re-test; don’t make multiple big changes at once if you want to know what actually helped.

Reliability & User Experience

  • Users will often operate the dispenser faster or harder than you do – design with that in mind.
  • Handles should be comfortable and provide enough leverage for the smallest or least-strong user in your target group.
  • Guards and covers should prevent fingers from reaching pinch points while still allowing the mechanism to be seen.

Common Mistakes

  • Blaming the lollies or “bad luck” instead of looking for a design cause (geometry, finish, alignment).
  • Not recording changes, so you forget what you altered or cannot undo a change that made things worse.
  • Ignoring user feedback because “it works for me” – engineering solutions need to work for others, not just the designer.
Quiz

Check Your Understanding – Mechanism Testing & Tuning

Use these questions to test your understanding. Click “Show answer” to reveal one possible solution or explanation.

1. Your dispenser works well when it is half full, but jams frequently when almost empty. Give one possible reason.
When the dispenser is nearly empty, there may not be enough weight or pressure to feed lollies consistently into the gate. They may sit in corners or flat spots instead of rolling into position. Adjusting the slope, surface finish or hopper shape can help guide them correctly, even at low levels.
2. Explain how changing the gear ratio could make the dispenser easier to use for a younger child.
Increasing the gear ratio (more turns of the handle per cycle) can increase torque at the mechanism, which means less force is needed to turn the handle. The trade-off is that the user may need to turn the handle further, but it will feel lighter and more controllable for a younger user.
3. What does it mean to “change one variable at a time” during testing, and why is it important?
It means you only adjust one thing (e.g. a clearance, spring tension or surface finish) between tests. If you change multiple things at once, you cannot tell which change caused the improvement (or the problem). Changing one variable at a time makes your results more reliable and easier to explain.
4. Give two examples of data you could collect in a 10-cycle test that would help you judge reliability.
Examples:
  • Number of successful dispenses out of 10.
  • Number of jams or partial dispenses out of 10.
  • Number of double dispenses (more than one lolly released).
  • Approximate handle force or number of “stiff” points per cycle.
Any two of these, recorded systematically, help you compare before/after changes.
5. A user reports that the handle feels “jerky” at one point each turn. What are two potential causes you might investigate?
Possible causes include:
  • A high spot or misalignment on one gear tooth or cam surface.
  • Insufficient clearance where a cam or gate contacts another part.
  • A bent shaft or off-centre hole causing uneven rotation.
You would inspect these areas visually and by slowly rotating the mechanism to confirm.
6. Why is user feedback an important part of mechanism testing, even if the mechanism “works” in your own trials?
Users may have different strength, hand size and expectations. They might operate the mechanism differently (faster, slower, at an angle), revealing issues you did not notice. Feedback helps you design a dispenser that is comfortable, safe and reliable for a wider range of people, not just the designer.
