Materials

Learning Focus

By the end of this toolkit, you should be able to:

  • Compare solid timber and sheet products (ply, MDF, particleboard, acrylic) for use in the geared lolly dispenser.
  • Select appropriate materials for frames, guards and moving parts based on strength, stability, machinability and finish quality.
  • Plan efficient nesting and cut lists that minimise waste and keep parts referenced to the same datum.
  • Apply safe working procedures for sawing, drilling, sanding and edge preparation of timber and sheet goods.
  • Choose and apply suitable surface finishes and explain the sustainability of your material and finish choices.

Core Concepts

Timber & Sheet Goods for the Dispenser

This toolkit links directly to the Term 4 geared lolly dispenser build. The focus is on choosing the right material for each job and understanding how that choice affects safety, accuracy, durability and sustainability.

Solid Timber

  • Advantages: Strong in bending, looks good, can be repaired and refinished, good for visible parts (front frame, side panels, handles).
  • Limitations: Moves with humidity (expands/contracts), can twist or bow, grain direction matters around holes and narrow strips.
  • Best uses: Frames and parts where grain can be aligned with the load or longest span; parts that are regularly handled.

Plywood & Other Sheet Products

  • Plywood: Layers with alternating grain directions – more stable, less likely to cup or twist; good for backs, bases, guard panels and jigs.
  • MDF / particleboard: Flat and smooth for painting; edges are weak and dusty when machined; best in internal panels, not exposed edges.
  • Acrylic / clear sheet: Useful for guards so users can see gears; must be de-burred and supported to avoid cracking.

Moisture, Movement & Stability

  • Timber takes in and loses moisture – this can cause bowing, cupping and joint gaps if not considered in your design.
  • Long, thin strips with short grain around holes are most at risk of snapping or twisting out of alignment.
  • Plywood is generally more dimensionally stable, but edges must be sealed to prevent moisture uptake and splintering.

Sustainability & Ethical Choices

  • Prefer plantation-grown species and products with certification where possible.
  • Use nesting and cut planning to reduce waste; design parts to use offcuts from other components when sensible.
  • Choose finishes with lower VOCs (volatile organic compounds) and plan for safe disposal of rags and solvents.
Workshop Practice

Practical Sequence & Checkpoints

Use this sequence as a guide through the practical work. Tick off checkpoints as you go and record evidence in your folio.

  1. Read the working drawing and exploded view.
    • Highlight which parts are structural (frames, base) and which are mainly cosmetic (front panel, trims).
    • Decide which parts are best in solid timber vs sheet products and note your reasons.
  2. Create a cut list and nesting plan.
    • List each part, quantity, material, thickness and finished dimensions.
    • Sketch a nesting layout on the timber or sheet showing grain direction and part orientation.
    • Leave safe margins for the saw kerf and clamping.
  3. Prepare and test on scrap.
    • Use scrap pieces to test saw settings, drill sizes, countersinks and sanding before cutting real parts.
    • Check fit of joints (e.g., dowels, screws, rebates) on scrap to minimise mistakes in finished pieces.
  4. Cut, drill, label and store parts.
    • Cut in logical order, starting with the longest/straightest reference edges.
    • Clearly label all parts with pencil (name, orientation, reference face) and store flat to avoid warping.
    • Keep offcuts sorted – usable strips and panels in one box, true waste in another for bin/compost.
  5. Dry assemble and check alignment.
    • Assemble without glue first; check frames are square and moving parts have clearance.
    • Shim or adjust if the frame has twisted or if parts bind when rotating.
  6. Surface preparation and finishing.
    • Sand with the grain; progressively move through grits as required (e.g., 120 → 180 → 240).
    • Seal exposed end grain and ply edges first; wipe off dust before applying finish.
    • Record the type of finish (oil, lacquer, paint) and safety requirements in your folio.

Key Notes

Key Notes & Common Mistakes

Material Selection & Structure

  • Ply vs solid: Plywood generally stays flatter; solid timber can move with humidity and twist frames or guards if badly chosen.
  • Sheet thickness: Use the thinnest sheet that is still stiff enough. Too thick adds unnecessary weight and inertia to moving parts.
  • Grain direction: Align grain along the longest span. Avoid holes near short grain corners where parts are likely to snap.

Guards, Jigs & Interfaces

  • Use thin ply or acrylic for guards; avoid sharp corners and leave clear sight lines to gears and cams.
  • Design simple jigs from offcuts to hold repetitive parts while drilling or sanding.
  • Allow clearance around rotating parts and fixings so nothing rubs once assembled.

Common Mistakes

  • Cutting parts before planning the nesting, leading to wasted material and parts that do not reference from the same straight edge.
  • Ignoring grain direction and then wondering why parts twist or split around screw holes.
  • Leaving ply/MDF edges unsealed so they swell, fluff up or chip during use.
  • Forcing warped parts into the frame instead of re-cutting or bracing them, which builds twist into the whole dispenser.
Quiz

Check Your Understanding – Timber Materials

Use these questions to test yourself. Click “Show answer” to reveal one possible solution or explanation.

1. Why might you choose plywood instead of solid pine for the back panel and base of the dispenser?
Plywood is more dimensionally stable and less likely to warp or twist, especially in larger flat panels. It keeps the whole frame squarer over time and still has good strength in bending, making it ideal for backs and bases.
2. What are two disadvantages of using MDF for exposed edges and high-wear parts?
MDF edges are weak and crumbly when machined, so they damage easily, and they soak up moisture and finish very quickly, often giving a rough or “fluffy” edge unless heavily sealed. It is better suited to hidden panels that will be fully painted.
3. Explain why nesting parts tightly on a board can improve both sustainability and accuracy.
Nesting reduces waste because you use more of the board and throw out fewer offcuts. It also keeps parts referenced from the same straight edge and datum, so dimensions are more consistent and joints line up more easily in assembly.
4. A long frame piece is slightly bowed after cutting. Give two reasonable options for dealing with this.
Possible options include:
  • Re-cut the part from straighter stock and use the bowed piece as scrap or for smaller components.
  • Brace the bowed piece as a non-critical internal part where the bow will not affect movement or alignment.
Avoid forcing the bowed piece into the frame, as it builds twist into the whole dispenser.
5. Why is it important to seal end grain and ply edges before or during finishing?
End grain and ply edges absorb finish much faster than flat grain surfaces. If you do not seal them, they become rough and patchy, and they can take in more moisture over time, leading to swelling, splintering and a lower-quality, less durable finish.
6. Give one example of a more sustainable material or design decision you could make for the dispenser.
Example answers might include:
  • Using plantation-grown pine or certified ply instead of an unsourced hardwood.
  • Designing parts to reuse offcuts from other components instead of cutting from fresh stock.
  • Choosing a low-VOC finish and applying it carefully so you do not waste product.
