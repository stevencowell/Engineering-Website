Evaluation

Learning Focus

By the end of this toolkit, you should be able to:

  • Evaluate your geared lolly dispenser against agreed design criteria and constraints.
  • Use evidence (tests, data, photos, user feedback) to support your evaluation statements.
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses in your design, construction and testing process.
  • Propose realistic future improvements and justify them using engineering language.
  • Present a clear, organised folio that communicates your process from concept to tested product.

Core Concepts

Design Criteria, Evidence & Reflection

This toolkit focuses on how you judge the success of your dispenser and how well you communicate your learning in your folio.

Design Criteria & Constraints

  • Function: Does it reliably dispense the intended quantity of lollies when operated correctly?
  • Safety: Are moving parts guarded, and are pinch points minimised?
  • Ergonomics: Is the handle comfortable, and is the required effort suitable for the target user?
  • Durability: Does it survive repeated use without loosening, cracking or wearing out quickly?
  • Aesthetics & finish: Is it visually appealing, with a consistent and appropriate finish?
  • Sustainability: Were material and process choices reasonably sustainable?

Evidence-Based Judgements

  • Use data (success rates, number of jams, user feedback) to support each evaluation statement.
  • Refer to specific tests from Toolkit 3 and decisions from Toolkits 1–2.
  • Avoid generic comments like “it’s good” – explain how you know it performs well (or not).
  • Include photos or diagrams that clearly show features you are discussing.

Reflection & Improvement

  • Reflection is more than listing steps – it explains what worked, what did not, and what you would change.
  • Improvements should be realistic (could actually be built in a school workshop) and linked to a problem you observed.
  • Use engineering language: refer to forces, materials, mechanisms, tolerances and clearances where relevant.
Folio

Folio Structure & Checkpoints

Use this structure as a guide for your final folio or digital submission. Some sections may already exist from earlier terms.

  1. Front cover & overview.
    • Project title, your name, class and year.
    • 1–2 sentences describing the purpose of the dispenser and the target user.
  2. Design brief & criteria.
    • Short design brief explaining the problem being solved.
    • Dot-point list of design criteria and constraints (function, safety, ergonomics, aesthetics, sustainability, etc.).
  3. Key stages summary.
    • Brief outline of major stages (materials selection, CAD modelling, prototyping, testing).
    • Use 1–2 images per stage with captions rather than long blocks of text.
  4. Testing & results (link to Toolkit 3).
    • Include your main test table (success/jam/double dispense data).
    • Add at least one graph or clearly presented table if possible.
    • Summarise key findings in a few sentences.
  5. Evaluation against criteria.
    • For each criterion, write a short paragraph stating how well your design meets it.
    • Refer to specific evidence (data, photos, user comments) in each paragraph.
  6. Improvements & future development.
    • List at least two realistic improvements (design or process) explaining why each would help.
    • Optionally sketch a quick concept or annotate a photo showing where the improvement would be made.
  7. Personal reflection.
    • Reflect on what you learned about mechanisms, materials and CAD this year.
    • Describe at least one challenge you faced and how you worked through it.
Activities & Evidence

Activities & Evidence

These items should be clearly visible in your folio or attached to your final submission.

  • Completed evaluation table or set of paragraphs addressing each key design criterion.
  • At least one annotated photo of the finished dispenser, labelling important design or safety features.
  • A copy of your test data summary from Toolkit 3, linked to your evaluation statements.
  • A short improvements list (2–4 dot points) with brief justification for each improvement.
  • A 1–2 paragraph personal reflection on what you learned and how your skills developed.
  • All major sections of the folio are in a logical order with headings and page numbers (if printed).
Presentation

Presentation & Communication

Visual Communication

  • Use clear, well-lit photos; avoid cluttered backgrounds.
  • Crop images so the dispenser and key features are the focus.
  • Use simple annotations (arrows, labels) instead of long paragraphs where possible.

Written Communication

  • Write in short paragraphs and use headings/subheadings.
  • Use correct engineering terms (e.g. torque, clearance, cam, gear ratio) where appropriate.
  • Avoid slang; be clear and specific rather than vague.

Marking Readiness Check

  • Can someone who was not in your class understand what you built, why and how it performs?
  • Is there enough evidence to justify the mark you think you deserve?
  • Are key documents (drawings, test tables, evaluation paragraphs) easy to find and read?
Key Notes

Key Notes & Common Mistakes

Stronger Evaluations

  • Good evaluations connect criteria + evidence + judgement.
  • Use phrases like “because”, “as shown in”, and “this means that” to link your points.
  • Being honest about weaknesses can show deeper understanding than pretending everything is perfect.

Common Mistakes

  • Only describing what you did (a timeline) instead of judging how well it met the criteria.
  • Making big claims (“it’s perfect”, “never jams”) with no test data or user feedback to back them up.
  • Leaving improvements too vague (“make it better”, “use stronger material”) without specifics.
  • Submitting a folio with missing sections or unlabelled photos, making it hard to follow your process.
Quiz

Check Your Understanding – Evaluation & Folio

Use these questions to test your understanding. Click “Show answer” to reveal one possible solution or explanation.

1. What is the difference between describing your project and evaluating it?
Describing your project tells the story of what you did (steps, tools, timeline). Evaluating your project judges how well it met the design criteria using evidence (tests, data, feedback) and explains why. A strong folio includes both, but evaluation focuses on quality and performance, not just process.
2. Give an example of a weak evaluation statement and then improve it using evidence.
Weak: “My dispenser works well and doesn’t jam.”

Improved: “My dispenser met the reliability criterion in 9 out of 10 test cycles, with only one minor jam when the hopper was almost empty. This suggests it is generally reliable, but feed shape and slope could still be improved for low-fill conditions.”
3. Why is it useful to link evaluation comments back to specific design criteria?
Linking comments to specific criteria shows that you are judging the design against the agreed success measures, not random opinions. It keeps your evaluation organised and makes it easier for a teacher or marker to see how well you understood and met the brief.
4. Your dispenser is reliable but hard to turn for younger users. Give one realistic improvement and justify it.
One improvement could be to increase the gear ratio so the handle turns further but requires less force. This would keep the same basic mechanism but make it easier for younger users to operate, improving ergonomics without reducing reliability.
5. What makes an annotated photo more useful than an unlabelled one in a folio?
An annotated photo directs the viewer’s attention to specific features or issues with labels and arrows. It explains why the image is important (e.g. showing a safety guard, clearance, or improvement), instead of forcing the reader to guess what they are meant to notice.
6. Why can honestly identifying weaknesses and future improvements actually strengthen your overall evaluation?
It shows that you understand engineering trade-offs and can think critically about your own work. Recognising limitations and suggesting realistic improvements demonstrates deeper learning than pretending everything is perfect, and it shows how you would move towards a more refined solution next time.
