Structures

Learning Focus

  • Explore tensegrity (tensional integrity) where compression struts are suspended by a network of tensioned cables.
  • Compare tension versus compression forces and link them to material properties and design choices.
  • Identify suitable materials for struts and cables, considering preload, equilibrium and safety.

Key Notes

Why engineers care about tensegrity

Engineers design structures so loads flow safely to the ground. In a timber frame, beams and columns touch and share compression; in a tensegrity, isolated compression struts are suspended by tensioned cables so none of the struts touch. Stability comes from the balance of continuous tension and discrete compression, making tensegrity a powerful visual for understanding equilibrium and the path of forces.

Forces, equilibrium and stability in tensegrity

When you look at any structure, ask three questions: what forces act on it, how are they balanced, and what keeps it stable if something changes? In tensegrity, struts carry compression while cables carry tension, and the forces at every joint must add to zero for equilibrium. Pre-tensioned cables stiffen the shape, resisting small disturbances, and material choices deliberately separate compression members (stiff dowels or tubes) from tension elements (high-tensile cords).

Materials in tension and compression

Compression members need stiffness to avoid buckling, so straight, rigid struts matter just as much as the material itself. Tension members need high tensile strength and low stretch so preload holds. Try experiments: compare cotton string to braided fishing line under the same load, or test how quickly a thin skewer buckles compared with a thicker dowel of the same length to see how geometry affects stability.

From theory to the phone-stand project

The tensegrity phone stand follows an engineering process: define the problem (hold a phone at eye level), analyse the loads, pick a triangular prism form, then select materials based on your tension and compression tests. As you build, link each decision to forces and materials—how does changing strut length, cable type or drilling angle alter the balance? Troubleshoot by observing where joints twist or cables slacken, then refine until the stand holds the phone steadily.

Safety, risk and sustainability

Apply Stage 5 risk management by identifying hazards when cutting and drilling struts or tensioning cables. Use eye protection, clamp workpieces and keep the workspace tidy to prevent trips and cuts. Sustainability connects directly to tensegrity because it achieves strength with minimal material; compare renewable options like bamboo to plastics or metals and discuss embodied energy, waste and opportunities to reuse offcuts.

Activities & Evidence

  • Build a three-strut tensegrity prism, record preload steps and photograph the final model from multiple angles.
  • Measure load and deflection for the prism, noting when the structure distorts or cables slacken.
  • Test tensile and compressive strength of sample materials to justify strut and cable choices.
  • Extension: design a four- or six-strut tensegrity or model the structure in a simulation tool.

Q&A

What balance of forces keeps a tensegrity structure stable?
Stability comes from compression in the struts being balanced by continuous tension in the cables. Preload sets the initial balance so small movements are resisted and the struts stay separated.
Why should struts never touch?
If struts touch, the force path changes, friction can appear and loads bypass the tension network. This removes the tensional integrity and can cause buckling or sudden collapse.
How does preload affect stability?
Preload tightens the cable network so the structure holds its geometry before external loads are applied. Too little preload leads to slack cables; too much can overstress connections or bend struts.
Which materials work best for cables?
Use low-stretch cord or braided fishing line that holds knots securely. Soft string creeps under load, while wire needs careful bending and protection against fraying or sharp ends.
