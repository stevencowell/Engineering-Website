Toolkit 3: Geared Lolly Dispenser

This toolkit extends students from simple machines to compound mechanisms using a geared lolly dispenser project. It links Stage 5 outcomes in mechanisms, materials, safety, sustainability and communication, with an emphasis on testing, mechanical advantage and user-focused design.

Mechanisms

Learning Focus

  • Move from isolated gear pairs or levers to compound mechanisms that combine gears, cams, ratchets and springs.
  • Design a geared lolly dispenser that stores sweets securely and releases a set number per handle turn.
  • Plan, build and test reliable motion sequences that are safe, ergonomic and easy to operate.
  • Use mechanical analysis (gear ratios, mechanical advantage and efficiency) to improve dispenser performance.

Key Notes

From simple to compound mechanisms

The dispenser project moves beyond single levers or gear pairs and into compound mechanisms. Students combine multiple simple machines — gears, cams, ratchets, pawls and springs — into a single system that can store lollies, release them in a controlled way and stop back-driving under load.

Gear trains and control of motion

Gears can slow a fast-turning handle, increase torque to turn a loaded drum and synchronise motion between parts. In a two-stage train, students calculate the overall ratio by multiplying each stage, then match the product to the drum rotation needed for one or two serves per handle turn. This gives a concrete context for discussing speed reduction, torque increase and direction of rotation.

Mechanical advantage and efficiency

Mechanical advantage is the ratio between the useful output (turning a loaded drum) and the input effort at the handle. Students explore this qualitatively by comparing how hard the handle feels with different gear ratios, and can extend this with simple measurements using a spring scale at the handle. Designs are compared by how consistently they deliver the required number of lollies with reasonable effort.

Efficiency is linked to friction and losses in the system. Poor alignment, rough bearing surfaces or tight clearances waste effort as heat, noise and wear rather than useful motion. Encouraging students to improve alignment, choose appropriate materials and apply lubrication where appropriate provides a natural context for discussing efficiency.

Cams, followers and timing of the gate

Radial cams (heart or egg shapes) give smooth lifts, while snail or drop cams give long dwells and sudden drops. Students link the cam profile to the timing of the gate so that it opens only when the drum aligns with the chute and closes firmly to stop extra lollies escaping. This highlights how changing a cam profile changes motion type and timing.

Reliability, jamming and tolerance

Real lollies are not identical. To design for slightly different sizes, students add clearances to the drum pockets and chute and then test prototypes with different lolly types. They log jams, misfeeds, the number dispensed per operation and any wear, then adjust slot size, chamfers, surface finish and material choice to improve reliability.

Aesthetics, user interaction and ethics

Handles should be ergonomic with no pinch points, and viewing windows and simple instructions help users understand how to operate the dispenser. Students briefly consider hygiene, sugar consumption, accessibility and material sustainability, then document how the design meets user needs and ethical expectations.

Risk management in dispenser construction

Students identify risks from cutting and drilling, small loose parts and pinch points around moving components. They apply hierarchy-of-control thinking to propose guards, enclosed gearboxes, clamps for drilling, marked pinch zones and safe storage of small parts, reinforcing Stage 5 risk management outcomes.

Activities & Evidence

  • Sketch and prototype a geared lolly dispenser that stores lollies securely and releases a set amount per handle turn.
  • Calculate multi-stage gear ratios and explain how they change torque and mechanical advantage at the handle.
  • Measure and compare input effort (qualitative feel or simple force measurements) and discuss efficiency and mechanical advantage in different designs.
  • Design cam and follower profiles that time a gate to open only when the drum aligns, then return to a safe closed position.
  • Test prototypes with different lolly sizes, logging jams, number dispensed per operation and wear observations, then adjust the design to improve reliability.
  • Create a simple parts list and estimate material costs for one dispenser, comparing at least two material options (for example, ply versus acrylic).
  • Produce an orthogonal or CAD drawing of the dispenser mechanism (drum, cam, ratchet, gate) using Onshape or similar, suitable for manufacture.
  • Document ergonomics, hygiene choices, sustainability considerations and a project-specific risk assessment as part of the project folio.

Q&A

How can a compound mechanism stop the handle turning backwards?
Add a ratchet and pawl to the gear train. The ratchet wheel attaches to a driven shaft and the pawl allows rotation in one direction only. This stops the handle from back-driving under the weight of the lollies and protects the mechanism from accidental reverse loads.
How do you calculate the overall gear ratio for two stages?
Multiply the ratio of the first stage by the ratio of the second stage. For example, if stage one is 12:36 (1:3) and stage two is 10:40 (1:4), the overall ratio is 1:12. Students then match this product to the drum rotation they need per handle turn.
Why might a gate cam stay closed for most of the cycle?
Holding the gate closed (a long dwell) for most of the cycle helps prevent accidental spills. The cam only lifts the gate briefly when the dispensing drum is correctly aligned, then allows it to drop back to a secure closed position.
How do you design for lollies of slightly different sizes?
Allow for tolerances by making drum pockets and chutes slightly larger than the ideal lolly size. Students test with mixed lollies, note jams and misfeeds, then adjust clearances, chamfers and materials to balance reliable dispensing with control.
What does mechanical advantage look like in this project?
Mechanical advantage is seen when a small input effort at the handle can still turn a loaded drum smoothly. Higher gear ratios increase torque but reduce speed. Students can compare designs by how hard the handle feels to turn and how consistently the dispenser delivers the correct number of lollies.

Resources

  • Assorted spur and bevel gears, dowels or shafts, bearings or low-friction bushes, springs, rubber bands and plywood, acrylic or 3D-printed parts.
  • Cam blanks (card or MDF) for shaping profiles, followers, gates, ratchets and pawls.
  • Containers for lollies, guards or windows, small screws and adhesives; SWMS templates for cutting and drilling.
  • Datasheets or videos on compound gears, cam design, tolerances, ergonomic handle design and basic mechanical advantage.
  • Access to CAD software such as Onshape and a simple spreadsheet or calculator template for costing materials.
