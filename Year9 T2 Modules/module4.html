Learning Focus

By the end of this toolkit, you will be able to:

  • Cast concrete beams safely: Follow proper procedures for mixing, placing, and compacting concrete.
  • Manage curing: Maintain proper curing conditions for strength development.
  • Conduct destructive testing: Set up test equipment, apply loads systematically, and observe failure modes.
  • Collect and analyse data: Record loads, deflections, and failure characteristics, then analyse results.
  • Evaluate performance: Compare predicted vs actual performance, identify failure causes, and suggest improvements.

Timing: Cast your beam in Week 1-2, allow 7+ days for curing, then test in Week 3-4. Complete your beam folio by the end of Week 4. This is your main Term 2 project.

Concrete Beam – Casting Protocol

Timing: Cast beams in Week 1-2 based on your Toolkit 3 design. Beams need at least 7 days to cure before testing.

1) Mould preparation & reinforcement

  • Clean mould thoroughly; check dimensions match your design.
  • Apply release agent if required (light oil or commercial release agent).
  • Position reinforcement accurately using spacers or supports. Check cover dimensions (typically 15-20 mm minimum).
  • Secure reinforcement to prevent movement during concrete placement.

2) Mix and placement

  • Record the mix proportions (cement : sand : aggregate : water) exactly as used.
  • Mix dry ingredients (cement, sand, aggregate) thoroughly before adding water.
  • Add water gradually while mixing to achieve desired workability. Avoid adding too much water.
  • Place concrete in layers; tap or rod each layer to remove air bubbles and ensure compaction.
  • Fill mould completely, level the top surface, and smooth if required.
  • Label the beam with identification (group name, date, mix details).

3) Initial curing

  • Cover the mould to prevent moisture loss (plastic sheet, wet hessian, or curing compound).
  • Keep in a protected location, away from direct sunlight and wind.
  • Allow initial set (typically 24-48 hours) before demoulding.
  • Handle carefully when removing from mould - concrete is still relatively weak.

4) Continued curing

  • Keep beams moist and protected for the full curing period (minimum 7 days, ideally 28 days for maximum strength).
  • Note curing time and conditions (temperature, whether covered or not) in your folio.
  • Important: Beams are cast in Week 1-2 and need at least 7 days to cure before testing. Use this curing time to complete your design documentation and prepare test procedures. Testing occurs in Week 3-4.

Testing Protocol

1) Pre-test preparation

  • Record: date, group members, beam ID, dimensions, mix details, and curing time.
  • Inspect beam for obvious defects (cracks, surface irregularities, dimensions).
  • Set up supports at the specified span; ensure supports are level and stable.
  • Confirm the loading point(s) (central point or two-point loading) and loading mechanism.
  • Set up deflection measurement (ruler at mid-span, measured from a fixed datum).
  • Review SWMS and ensure all safety controls are in place.

2) Loading procedure

  • Add load in clear, recorded steps (e.g., 1 kg at a time, or consistent increments).
  • After each increment, pause to observe and record:
    • Total load applied
    • Deflection at mid-span
    • Any visible cracks or changes
    • Any sounds (pops, creaks)
  • Continue loading until failure or until deflection becomes excessive.
  • Use video to capture the whole test so you can pause and review exactly where and how failure started.

3) Failure observation

  • Record load at first crack (usually on tension side, bottom of beam).
  • Observe how crack pattern develops as load increases.
  • Record peak load reached before failure.
  • Note the failure mode:
    • Flexural failure (bending - most common)
    • Shear failure (diagonal cracks near supports)
    • Compression failure (crushing at top)
  • After failure, inspect the fracture: note tension cracking on underside, crushing at top, or shear near supports.
  • Take photos of the failure surface and crack pattern.

Data Collection & Analysis

Collecting useful data

  • Create a data table with columns for: load step, total load (N or kg), deflection (mm), observations.
  • Record all measurements consistently using the same units and methods.
  • Note any unusual observations (sounds, sudden changes, unexpected behaviour).
  • Take photos at key stages: before loading, at first crack, during loading, at failure, after failure.

Making sense of the results

  • Plot graphs where useful (e.g., load vs deflection). Look for:
    • Linear behaviour (elastic range)
    • Where behaviour changes (first cracking, yielding)
    • Peak load and failure point
  • Compare your results with other groups: did similar designs perform similarly? Why might there be differences?
  • Describe both strength (max load) and stiffness (deflection under load).
  • Link failure mode back to your design (reinforcement placement, mix strength, dimensions).

Analysis and evaluation

  • Compare predicted vs actual performance:
    • Did the beam fail at the expected load?
    • Did it fail in the expected mode?
    • What factors might explain differences?
  • Identify likely causes of failure:
    • Was reinforcement correctly placed?
    • Was the mix strong enough?
    • Were there construction defects?
    • Was curing adequate?
  • Suggest specific improvements for a re-design:
    • What would you change?
    • Why would this improve performance?
    • How would you test the improvement?

Folio Structure & Example

Important: Complete your concrete beam folio by the end of Week 4. This folio demonstrates your understanding of materials, design, construction, and testing.

Your folio should read like a short engineering report. A suggested structure is:

  • Cover & brief: Project title, group members, beam requirements, design brief summary.
  • Planning & safety: SWMS summary, key hazards and controls, tool and material lists.
  • Design & drawings: Beam design sketches, cross-sections, reinforcement layouts, mix design calculations.
  • Build log: Dated entries, photos of casting stages, problems encountered and how they were fixed.
  • Testing & data: Test setup, data tables, graphs, photos/stills, observations at first crack and failure.
  • Evaluation: Comparison of predicted vs actual performance, analysis of failure mode, specific improvements suggested. Link back to Toolkit 2 concepts (concrete as composite, reinforcement, curing).
  • Materials & sustainability: Materials brief, comments on material choice, environmental considerations.

Concrete Beam Test & Folio Q&A

Why do we cast and test a small concrete beam instead of just calculating a full-size design?

Real engineering always combines calculation, modelling and testing. A small beam lets you quickly try ideas, see unexpected failure modes, and practise measurement without the cost and risk of a full-size build.

Model testing helps you understand concrete behaviour, reinforcement action, and failure modes in a very direct way. Even though a small beam doesn't scale perfectly to full-size, the same principles apply: concrete cracks in tension, reinforcement carries tensile forces, and proper curing is essential.

What is the difference between strength and stiffness in your concrete beam?

Strength is about the maximum load the beam can carry before it fails (cracks, crushes, etc.).

Stiffness is about how much the beam deflects under everyday loads. A beam can be strong enough not to break, but still too flexible to feel safe or meet serviceability requirements.

In your evaluation, comment on both: "Our beam carried 450 N before failure, but deflected 8 mm at only 100 N, which would feel too flexible in real use."

Why do we test beams close to failure instead of stopping when they "look safe"?

Testing to or near failure shows the true capacity of the beam and reveals how it fails. This is vital for learning:

  • Where failure occurs (tension side, compression side, shear).
  • Whether failure is sudden (brittle) or gradual (ductile).
  • What design factors most affect performance.

In real engineering, we rarely load structures to failure, but we still need this information from prototypes and calculations so we can set realistic factors of safety.

What makes a good test method for your concrete beam?

A good test method is repeatable, controlled and safe:

  • Always loading at the same point (e.g., exact centre or specified two-point locations).
  • Adding load in clear, recorded steps (e.g., known masses, consistent increments).
  • Measuring deflection carefully (same ruler position, same datum).
  • Using appropriate PPE and a safe zone so no one is in the line of fire if it fails.

If other groups can repeat your test and get similar results, your method is strong.

Why might two beams with the same design fail at very different loads?

Small differences in construction quality can have a big effect:

  • Slightly different mix proportions or water content.
  • Reinforcement placement variations (cover, position).
  • Compaction differences (air voids reduce strength).
  • Curing conditions (moisture, temperature, duration).
  • Minor damage from handling before testing.

In your folio, it's useful to note these differences rather than assuming "the design was bad". Often the idea is sound, but the execution needs improvement.

What are the main sources of error in your beam tests?

Common error sources include:

  • Measurement error – reading rulers at an angle, misreading mass scales.
  • Load placement error – not exactly at mid-span or specified points.
  • Timing/step error – adding load too quickly, skipping recording steps.
  • Construction variation – the beam is not identical to the design.

Good evaluations mention these: "Deflection readings may have been 1–2 mm off due to ruler angle and movement while reading."

How can graphs and tables improve your folio?

Graphs and tables turn messy notes into clear evidence:

  • A table of load vs deflection lets you quickly see behaviour changes.
  • A load–deflection graph shows where behaviour changes from elastic to failing.
  • A comparison table of different groups' results highlights design factors.

When you write your evaluation, you can refer to these ("see Figure 1") instead of writing long lists of numbers.

What makes a strong evaluation paragraph in your folio?

A strong evaluation paragraph:

  • Starts with a clear judgement (e.g., "Overall, our beam was strong but too flexible.").
  • Supports that judgement with data (max load, deflection, failure location).
  • Links back to theory (concrete as composite, reinforcement action, curing effects).
  • Finishes with specific improvements (more reinforcement, stronger mix, better curing).

Avoid vague comments like "we could have made it better"; say exactly what and why.

How should you talk about safety and environment in your evaluation?

Connect your project to real engineering responsibilities:

  • Mention key WHS controls you used (PPE, SWMS, safe handling of wet concrete, test safety).
  • Comment on material choice and environmental impact (cement production, aggregate sourcing, waste management).
  • Reflect on sustainability (concrete recycling, embodied energy, service life).

Even a small classroom project is a chance to practise thinking like an engineer who must keep people safe and limit environmental impact.

When you re-design, should you change everything at once?

It's usually better to change only the most critical features between versions:

  • Start with the factor that most affected failure (reinforcement placement, mix strength, dimensions).
  • Change one or two design parameters rather than everything.
  • Keep other factors constant so you can see what actually caused the improvement.

This makes it easier to see what actually caused the improvement. If you change everything at once, you learn less from the data.
