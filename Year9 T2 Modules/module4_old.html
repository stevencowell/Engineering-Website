Overview

Why engineering communication matters

Engineering is never just “building things”. Projects need a clear brief, a plan and a budget. They must be documented, evaluated and, if needed, redesigned. Materials costing, quantity calculations, Onshape drawings, vocabulary lists and research reports all feed outcomes like EGT5-COM-01 (communication), EGT5-ENV-01 (sustainability) and EGT5-USE-01 (materials selection).

Drawings

Interpreting and producing engineering drawings

  • Read orthographic drawings of the water tower, concrete beam, digger and dispenser, identifying views, dimensions and symbols.
  • Create or edit multi-view Onshape drawings with title blocks, scales and annotations.
  • Apply Australian dimensioning standards where practical: centre lines, hidden detail, tolerances and surface finishes.
  • Emphasise clarity so another person can manufacture the part or assembly without guesswork.

Students see how inconsistent reference points or missing dimensions can cause parts not to fit.

Costing

Costing and simple project management

  • Use spreadsheets to calculate quantities (timber lengths, screw counts, syringes, tubing) and extended costs.
  • Add waste allowances and contingencies, then compare two design options on total cost and durability.
  • Build simple formulas with cell references so changing a unit price updates the whole sheet.

Introduce milestones, simple Gantt-style schedules and tracking progress versus plan to show that engineering decisions are technical and economic.

Folio

Folios and engineering reports

What to include

  • Design brief and constraints: safety, size limits, budget, materials and timeline.
  • Research: reference images, notes on mechanisms, industry examples and material data.
  • Concept sketches with pros/cons and a justified final choice.
  • Working drawings or CAD prints, plus a production log for each lesson.
  • Testing data, evaluation and reflection with potential improvements.

Why it matters

This folio structure mirrors how engineers communicate with clients, contractors and regulators, and aligns with Stage 5 expectations for communicating ideas, concepts and solutions.

Sustainability

Sustainability and lifecycle thinking

  • Compare environmental impacts of metals, plastics, timber and composites.
  • Consider lifecycle: disassembly, reuse and recycling when the project ends.
  • Discuss energy use, battery life and charging or alternative energy options.
  • Complete a simple lifecycle review listing material sources, processes, use phase and end-of-life options to support EGT5-ENV-01.
Careers

Careers and pathways

  • Research what mechanical, civil, mechatronic and industrial engineers do day-to-day.
  • Link class mechanisms (e.g. lolly dispenser) to industry equipment like packaging lines or mining machines.
  • Map training paths such as TAFE, apprenticeships and university degrees to the technologies used in class.
