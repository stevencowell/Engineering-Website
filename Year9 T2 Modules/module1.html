Key Notes

Why engineers care about tensegrity

Engineers design structures so loads flow safely to the ground. In conventional frames (like trusses or towers) some members are in compression, others are in tension, and joints are designed so forces balance. Tensegrity structures are a special case: isolated compression members (struts) do not touch, but are held in place only by a continuous network of tensioned cables. This makes tensegrity a powerful visual for understanding equilibrium and the path of forces.

Forces, equilibrium and stability in tensegrity

When you look at any structure, ask three questions: what forces are acting, how do they flow from one member to another, and what stops the structure from changing shape? In tensegrity, struts carry compression while cables carry tension. The whole structure is in pre-tension: cables are tightened so that, even before the phone is added, the system is already under load. Stability comes from the balance of these internal forces with external loads such as the weight of the phone and gravity. If a cable is too loose, the load path changes and the structure will distort or collapse.

Materials overview and classification

Before choosing materials for the tensegrity stand, engineers classify them into broad groups such as metals and alloys (for example aluminium tube), timbers (such as pine dowel), polymers (including nylon fishing line and braided cords) and composites (materials that combine two or more distinct components). Each group has characteristic properties: hardness, toughness, ductility and malleability describe how a material responds when bent or hammered, while tensile and compressive strength and stiffness describe how it behaves under pulling and squashing forces. In this project students should identify which group each candidate material belongs to and which properties matter most for that part of the structure, then use test results to justify their final choices.

Materials in tension and compression

Compression members need stiffness to avoid buckling, so straight, short struts made from timber dowel or aluminium tube perform well. Tension members need high tensile strength and low stretch so preload holds. Try experiments: compare a soft cotton string and a braided nylon line under the same load, or test a thin dowel versus a thicker dowel of the same length to see how geometry affects stability.

From theory to the phone-stand project

The tensegrity phone stand follows an engineering process: define the problem (support a phone at a comfortable viewing angle), analyse loads, sketch several possible arrangements, choose a simple triangular prism form, then select materials based on your tension and compression tests. Build a prototype, observe how it behaves as you add the phone and gently bump the table, adjust cable lengths if members twist or cables slacken, then refine until the stand holds the phone steadily. As an extension, students can compare this process with engineered structures developed by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, such as traditional fish traps or shelters, identifying how natural materials are used in tension and compression.

Safety, risk and sustainability

Apply Stage 5 risk management by identifying hazards when cutting and drilling struts, tensioning cables and working with adhesives. Wear safety glasses, clamp workpieces and keep the workspace tidy to prevent trips and cuts. From a sustainability perspective, consider using renewable materials (such as plantation pine or bamboo) and minimising offcuts by nesting parts when marking out. Discuss embodied energy, waste and opportunities to reuse offcuts.

Activities & Evidence

  • Build a three-strut tensegrity prism, record preload steps and photograph the final model from multiple angles.
  • Measure load and deflection for the prism, noting when the structure distorts or cables slacken.
  • Test tensile and compressive strength of sample materials to justify strut and cable choices.
  • Prepare a one-page engineering log or mini-report that classifies the materials used, presents your test results in a table or simple graph, and evaluates how well the tensegrity structure met the design criteria. You can build on this in Toolkit 4, where you will develop more detailed drawings and costing for your chosen project.
  • Extension: design a four- or six-strut tensegrity or model the structure in a simulation tool.

Q&A

What balance of forces keeps a tensegrity structure stable?
Stability comes from compression in the struts being balanced by tension in the cables. The cables are pre-tensioned so that, even before external loads are applied, they pull the system into a stable shape. When the phone is added, this internal balance adjusts slightly but remains in equilibrium as long as no member exceeds its strength or buckles.
Why should struts never touch?
If struts touch, they create new compression paths that bypass the cable network. This changes the way forces flow through the structure and can remove the tensegrity behaviour altogether. Keeping struts separated ensures cables remain the only connections, so tension and compression members do their distinct jobs.
What properties make a good cable material?
Good cable materials have high tensile strength, low stretch under load, and good knot-holding or fixing ability. In classroom projects, braided nylon fishing line or strong polyester cord works well. Cotton string stretches and wears quickly, so it may not maintain preload and can lead to instability.
How could you improve the sustainability of the phone stand?
Options include using renewable or recycled materials (for example bamboo or plantation timber), minimising offcuts by careful nesting, designing joints so the stand can be disassembled and parts reused, and avoiding unnecessary finishes that add waste and embodied energy. Students can also consider whether a tensegrity stand is the best solution or if a simpler structure could achieve the same function with fewer resources.
