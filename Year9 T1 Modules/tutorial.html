Plan the Build

Link every sketch to forces: which members are in compression (risk of buckling) and which are in tension (check joints).

Tower layout

  • Sketch front/top views with member lengths, base footprint, and bracing pattern. Keep bays consistent to avoid twists.
  • Triangulate every panel; avoid long unbraced columns. Add cross-bracing near the base where moments are highest.
  • Keep weight low; shorten compression members and use tension ties where possible.

Jointing & Assembly

Joints

  • Use gussets or double laps rather than single-point glue. Spread load across the joint area.
  • Keep opposing faces parallel; avoid gaps that cause racking. Dry-fit first, then glue.
  • Stage assembly symmetrically (build pairs) to control twist and keep plumb.

Quality checks

  • Pre-cut identical members in batches; label parts; keep offcuts for spares.
  • Measure base diagonals; adjust before glue cures so the frame sits flat.

Test Setup

  • Review SWMS and PPE. Clear fall zones; set clamps/fixtures and loading plate/bucket centrally.
  • Add load in steps; note load at first deflection and at failure. Use steady increments for clean data.
  • Film side and front views; mark reference points for deflection if possible.

Evidence to Capture

  • Photos: build stages, joints, final build, test setup, failure close-up.
  • Data: mass of tower/beam, load steps, peak load, estimated deflection, material list.
  • Reflection: what failed first, why (buckling, joint peel, shear), one change to improve stiffness/weight/corrosion protection.

Quick Q&A

Why triangulate every side of the tower?
Triangulation stops racking and shortens compression members, reducing buckling risk and keeping loads axial.
Where do you expect the highest compression in the tower?
Near the base, especially in the longest verticals under the loading point; add bracing and keep them short.
What should you record during load testing?
Load steps, first visible deflection, peak load, and failure mode (member buckling, joint shear, material crack).
How do you minimise glue joint failures?
Use gussets/double laps, ensure full contact with even glue, clamp while curing, and avoid peel by aligning members with load paths.
